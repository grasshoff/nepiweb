Overview

This chapter offers a critical examination of the current state and future trajectory of Large Language Models (LLMs). It advocates for the establishment of a novel discipline, computational epistemology, to address the inherent limitations of LLMs, particularly concerning validation and the generation of justified knowledge. The authors highlight the rapid evolution of LLMs, from foundational ‘attention’ mechanisms to the integration of ‘context’ and ‘thinking’ capabilities. Simultaneously, they identify crucial missing elements, such as robust hallucination countermeasures and the capacity for genuine scientific inquiry.

The authors propose a multi-component infrastructure designed to facilitate epistemic agency. This infrastructure encompasses a scholarly curated editorial board, exemplified by the comprehensive Opera Omnia Euler, which ensures the reliability of historical sources. Crucially, a Scholarium database, serving as an alternative to embedding-based approaches, systematically organises validated historical activities, chronologies, and communications. The technical backbone leverages multimodal LLMs, including Gemini 2.5, Claude, and Llama, within a Cursor environment. This setup enables an AI agent, Bernoulli, to conduct complex historical inquiries. Furthermore, the project ensures long-term data preservation through a FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) repository, such as Zenodo, hosted by CERN. It also establishes standardised API access to knowledge via an MCP API server, supported by Open Science Technology. This holistic approach aims to move beyond mere information retrieval, providing demonstrably justified and complete answers, particularly vital for rigorous historical and scientific research.

8.1 Evolution of Large Language Models

The domain of Large Language Models has undergone a remarkably swift evolution. Initially, the foundational ‘Attention is all you need’ paradigm propelled their development. Subsequently, the research team shifted its focus towards incorporating broader context, leading to the ‘Context is all you need’ approach, exemplified by techniques such as Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). Presently, the latest models propose ‘Thinking is all you need’ as a further advancement in LLM capabilities. Nevertheless, a critical question persists: do these advancements truly fulfil all requirements, or do fundamental elements remain absent from the current technological landscape?

8.2 Addressing Limitations in Large Language Models

A significant deficiency within current Large Language Models lies in their lack of a robust mechanism for countering hallucinations. Crucially, embedding vectors, whilst useful for representing linguistic patterns, do not encapsulate genuine meaning; rather, they merely capture impressions or expressions. Consequently, LLMs risk formulating statements that sound plausible yet are factually incorrect. They might also inadvertently disseminate unverified content from internet media, which does not constitute reliable knowledge. Instead, the authors contend that systems ought to provide demonstrably justified information and facilitate the planning of rigorous scientific inquiry. Presently, no existing model possesses these capabilities, nor does the current technological trajectory offer a clear path towards achieving these essential, yet missing, objectives.

8.3 The Imperative of Validation and Computational Epistemology

Comprehensive validation stands as a paramount requirement for robust knowledge systems. This process necessitates the provision of compelling reasons, well-structured arguments, and verifiable evidence, both supporting and refuting a given proposition’s veracity. Moreover, validation extends to assessing the rationale underpinning specific actions. To address this critical lacuna, the authors propose a new discipline: computational epistemology.

This emerging field specifically focuses on developing the methods and methodologies essential for achieving the requisite level of validation, thereby fostering epistemic agency. Achieving this agency demands the ability to identify propositions that transcend simple sentence structures. It also requires discerning arguments embedded within complex texts and historical sources, and accurately identifying the intentions, plans, and actions of individuals documented through their historical traces.

8.4 Practical Application: Historical Inquiry Workflow

The authors demonstrate the practical application of these principles through a working environment, depicted in a system screenshot. This platform facilitates historical inquiry, exemplified by a case study on the construction of Sanssouci Castle under Frederick the Great. A long-standing historical dispute centres upon the extent of Leonard Euler’s involvement in a significant 18th-century construction failure, specifically whether his contribution constituted a direct cause or merely an association with this failure.

The system allows users to formulate precise inquiries, such as ‘Reconstruiere welche Personen an der Wasserfontaine welche Arbeiten ausführten’ (Reconstruct which persons performed which work on the water fountain). The objective is to obtain a validated, qualified answer, rigorously supported by proven evidence, rather than relying solely on hearsay. The system successfully generates a detailed list of individuals, outlining their specific contributions, work periods, remuneration, and documented achievements or shortcomings. This working environment, known as Cursor, leverages AI agents; a specific agent, Bernoulli, is tasked with conducting these complex historical inquiries. A significant challenge, however, lies in effectively accessing and processing the entirety of available historical sources, as simple PDF indexing or token-based concentration proves insufficient for comprehensive analysis.

8.5 Core Components of a Validated Research Infrastructure

The authors’ proposed research infrastructure necessitates several core components, commencing with a scholarly curated editorial board. A prime example of such a resource is the Opera Omnia Euler, a monumental collection spanning 86 volumes. This extensive project, the culmination of 120 years of dedicated scholarly work by numerous experts, reached completion two years prior. It meticulously compiles all of Euler’s correspondence and his 866 publications. Furthermore, contributions from other scholars complement this work, collectively establishing it as a highly reliable source for detailed information concerning individuals.

8.6 The Scholarium: A Curated Content Database

A pivotal and innovative component of this infrastructure is the Scholarium, a novel scholarly source designed to supersede conventional embedding-based approaches. This system functions as a meticulously curated database of content items. It systematically organises chronologies of actions, records expressions communicated between individuals, tracks the evolution and use of terminology and language, and documents the historical application of tools and material objects. Crucially, every entry within the Scholarium undergoes rigorous validation against original historical sources. This meticulous process yields a highly detailed inventory of historically proven activities, each supported by verifiable historical records.

8.7 Leveraging Multimodal Large Language Models

Once these detailed historical records are established, the research team can effectively query them using readily accessible multimodal models. Experience indicates that multimodal models are optimally suited to address the complex requirements of such tasks. Specifically, the latest iterations of Gemini 2.5 prove particularly effective, given their inherent capability to seamlessly combine and process information derived from both textual and image-based sources.

8.8 Long-Term Data Preservation and Accessibility

Ensuring the enduring accessibility and reusability of research data necessitates a long-term FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) repository. For this purpose, the authors have selected Zenodo, hosted by CERN in Geneva. This platform provides the essential infrastructure for securely hosting the collected data over many years, thereby safeguarding its future utility and availability for the wider scholarly community.

8.9 Standardised API for Global AI Knowledge Access

Technical support for the infrastructure stems from Open Science Technology, a dedicated startup. This entity manages the operational aspects, including an MCP API server. Crucially, this server enables artificial intelligence models worldwide to access the curated data through a standardised API. This initiative represents a concerted effort to establish a global standard for AI access to knowledge, thereby fostering an environment of open collaboration within the research community.
