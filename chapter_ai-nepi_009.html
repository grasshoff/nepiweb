Overview

This chapter outlines an investigation into biases inherent within bibliometric databases, specifically employing large language models (LLMs) to assess the classification of publications related to the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). The authors initiated this work, recognising the critical influence of bibliometric databases on the sociology of science, impacting academics, funding institutions, and policymakers alike. Moreover, they acknowledged that these databases often reflect political and commercial interests, possessing a distinctly performative nature.

The investigation focused on three prominent databases: Web of Science, Scopus, and OpenAlex. Building upon prior research concerning SDG labelling and search query drafting, the project aimed to quantify the aggregate effect of metadata processing by these databases on research policy. A key objective involved employing LLMs to reclassify publications, thereby examining how changes in SDG-related research metadata might influence policy advice, choices, indicators, and measures.

The methodology encompassed the selection of five specific SDGs related to socio-economic inequalities—namely, education, gender equality, reducing inequalities, and an economic dimension. The authors applied this classification to a shared corpus of publications across the chosen databases. Initial findings corroborated earlier studies, revealing a minimal overlap in SDG labelling amongst the databases. To address this discrepancy, the authors developed an LLM-based approach, fine-tuning a pre-trained model on corpora classified under specific SDGs by each database, thereby creating a novel method for bias assessment.

9.1 Introduction and Research Motivation

The research team embarked on an ambitious project, aiming to leverage large language models (LLMs) for assessing inherent biases within publications classified across three major bibliometric databases. This endeavour stemmed from the authors’ profound understanding of the critical role these databases play in the sociology of science. Indeed, they exert significant influence over the behaviours of academics, researchers, funding institutions, and policymakers alike. Crucially, these platforms also respond to diverse political and commercial interests, possessing a distinctly performative nature that shapes the scientific landscape.

The investigation specifically focused on Web of Science, Scopus, and the recently open-sourced OpenAlex. Building upon prior research that explored the labelling of Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and the drafting of search queries, the authors observed a consistently minimal overlap in publications across these databases. This observation highlighted a potential aggregate effect stemming from the metadata processing within these systems, which ultimately influences the information available to researchers, policymakers, and consultants.

Consequently, the LLM project’s primary objective was to develop a general framework for assessing this aggregate effect, particularly its potential impact on research policy. The proposed chain of dependencies posits that LLMs, by altering the metadata associated with SDG-related research, could significantly influence subsequent advice, choices, indicators, and measures.

9.2 Data Sources and SDG Selection

The authors meticulously selected their data sources, focusing on three prominent bibliometric databases: the proprietary platforms Web of Science and Scopus, alongside the recently established open-source alternative, OpenAlex. To specifically model socio-economic inequalities, the research team strategically chose five Sustainable Development Goals. This selection encompassed three SDGs directly related to equity—namely, Education, Gender Equality, and Reducing Inequalities—complemented by an additional SDG addressing the economic dimension.

To ensure a consistent and comparable analysis, the authors compiled a shared corpus comprising all relevant publications. Rather than classifying entire databases, they applied their classification methodology directly to this shared corpus. This approach provided a robust benchmark, enabling a more precise assessment of classification discrepancies across the different platforms.

9.3 Initial Findings on SDG Labelling Overlap

The initial results from the classification process decisively aligned with Armitage’s (2020) findings, confirming a critical observation: the overlap in SDG labelling across the bibliometric databases proved remarkably small. This minimal concordance became strikingly evident upon examining the classified publications. For instance, a publication identified as SDG5 by one database might not receive the same classification from another; Scopus, for example, frequently omitted certain relevant publications from its SDG5 category.

Furthermore, a notable anomaly emerged within Web of Science’s classification of SDG5, where approximately 10% of the publications originated from mathematics, including topics such as geometrical differential equations. This unexpected inclusion suggests a potential misclassification or, at the very least, indicates a significant lack of contextual understanding within the automated labelling processes, highlighting areas where the current systems may be insufficient.

9.4 Large Language Model Implementation

To address the observed inconsistencies in SDG classification, the authors devised a novel approach centred on large language models. Initially, the research team considered training a dedicated LLM exclusively on corpora classified under a specific SDG by a particular bibliometric database.

However, recognising the substantial computational commitment this would entail, the authors opted for a more pragmatic compromise: fine-tuning a pre-trained LLM. This strategic decision aimed to achieve the overarching goal of assessing the aggregate effect of metadata processing and its subsequent impact on research policy, providing a flexible and efficient pathway to explore these complex interdependencies.
