Overview

This chapter details a project undertaken by the authors to develop and refine a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system for philosophical research, a direct response to the inherent limitations of standard Large Language Models (LLMs). Whilst models such as ChatGPT can generate plausible answers to philosophical queries, they exhibit critical flaws for academic use. They lack direct access to full-text sources, their training discourages verbatim recall, and they cannot reliably provide citations, often leading to hallucinated content.

The RAG architecture directly counters these issues by integrating a retrieval step into the generation process. This procedure involves searching a specialised corpus—such as the works of Aristotle or Einstein—for relevant text, which then augments the user’s prompt before it is submitted to the LLM. By grounding the model’s response in verifiable source material, this approach elegantly resolves the problems of access, context, and attribution.

A case study demonstrates this method by building a RAG system that uses the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (SEP) as its data source. Initial development revealed that a standard RAG implementation produced results inferior to the base LLM, necessitating extensive optimisation of hyperparameters and algorithms through a methodical trial-and-error process. A key finding emerged from the team’s analysis of text chunking strategies: for the highly structured SEP, using entire main sections as retrievable documents yielded the best results, even though their size exceeded the embedding model’s context limit.

The project concludes that whilst RAG systems are exceptionally well-suited for scientific and scholarly tasks, they require careful customisation and robust evaluation protocols. Future work points towards developing more flexible, agentic RAG systems capable of discerning between different question types, thereby overcoming challenges such as poor performance on broad overview queries.

12.1 RAG for Scholarly Inquiry

Standard Large Language Models, despite their fluency, exhibit critical deficiencies when applied to rigorous philosophical inquiry. An LLM can generate a differentiated response to a question like, ‘What is Aristotle’s theory of matter in the Physics?’, yet the answer remains untethered from the source material. To solve these fundamental problems, the authors developed a Retrieval-Augmented Generation system. The RAG architecture addresses three central limitations of standalone LLMs.

Firstly, it solves the access problem. LLMs are not designed to learn texts by heart; their training explicitly discourages verbatim reproduction in favour of learning generalisable rules of text production. Consequently, when asked to quote a specific passage, an LLM may refuse or, worse, hallucinate the text. Philosophical scholarship, in contrast, depends upon the ability to delve deeply into original sources and their precise formulations.

Secondly, RAG systems mitigate the challenge of a limited context window. Although modern models like ChatGPT 4.0 offer a 128,000-token context, this capacity is quickly overwhelmed by the large corpora common in humanities research. Finally, the RAG framework solves the problem of attribution. It enables the system to provide citations for its claims, satisfying the academic imperative for verifiable sources.

The workflow of a RAG system is straightforward yet powerful. It begins by retrieving relevant documents from a specified data source, such as the complete works of a philosopher, typically employing semantic search. The system then uses the most relevant text chunks it finds to augment the user’s original prompt. By providing this rich, factual context directly to the LLM, the RAG system ensures the final generated answer is not only intelligent but also grounded in and supported by the source documents.

12.2 Case Study: A RAG System for Philosophy

The project team initiated the development of a practical RAG system, aiming to enable users to ‘chat’ with philosophical corpora, such as the complete works of Locke, with far greater detail and fidelity than a general-purpose LLM allows. This technology holds considerable promise for both didactics and research. For students, it offers an intuitive way to approach dense texts, moving from general ideas to specific theories. For scholars, it can function as a powerful tool to look up facts in handbooks, explore unexamined text collections, locate passages for close reading, and, perhaps eventually, find detailed answers to parts of their research questions.

To test this potential, the authors created an example RAG system using the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (SEP) as the knowledge base, scraping its content into Markdown. The initial goal was to create a useful tool for the philosophical community. However, early experiments revealed a significant challenge: a textbook RAG implementation yielded answers that were surprisingly poor, often worse than querying GPT directly. This discovery shifted the project’s focus towards optimisation.

Improving the system became a process of methodical experimentation. The team began by adjusting models and their hyperparameters, eventually adding more complex algorithms like reranking to enhance the quality of retrieved results. This process was largely one of trial and error, where theoretical understanding guided the experiments, but empirical outcomes determined success. This work highlighted a crucial bottleneck: evaluation. In philosophy, answers are not atomic facts but complex, unstructured propositions. Assessing whether these propositions state the right facts is a difficult task that requires deep domain knowledge and robust evaluation standards.

12.3 System Architecture and Evaluation

The team constructed the SEP RAG system with a functional frontend interface and a backend powered by several thousand lines of Python code. The user interface provides a field for posing questions and includes controls for key hyperparameters, such as the desired prompt length and the number of documents to retrieve. A central feature of the design is its comparative evaluation framework, which facilitates rigorous assessment of the RAG system’s performance.

When a user submits a query, the system generates two responses and displays them side-by-side. On the left, it shows the benchmark answer from the base LLM operating alone. On the right, it presents the enhanced answer from the full RAG system. This direct comparison makes it easy to judge the value added by the retrieval-augmentation process.

Furthermore, the output includes a transparent list of the source texts found by the retrieval component. This list details the article names and section headings, and it clearly marks which texts were successfully included in the prompt and which were truncated due to length constraints. This design choice provides full visibility into the system’s internal operations.

12.4 Optimising Retrieval through Chunking

The authors conducted a focused investigation into optimising the ‘chunk size’ hyperparameter, a critical setting that dictates how source documents are divided for retrieval. Three primary strategies were considered a priori: using a fixed number of words, a common but semantically naive approach; chunking by paragraphs; and chunking by document sections. To some surprise, the investigation revealed that the best results were obtained by treating entire main sections of the SEP as single, retrievable documents.

This finding was unexpected because the average section length of 3,000 words far outstripped the embedding model’s effective input size of approximately 500 words. The team’s proposed explanation for this phenomenon lies in the unique structure of the data source. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy is a highly systematised work, and it appears the first 500 words of each main section contain a dense summary of its primary arguments. This allows the embedding model to capture the essence of the entire section and judge its relevance accurately.

Nevertheless, this successful strategy comes with a significant caveat: it is likely specific to this well-structured corpus and may not transfer to more heterogeneous texts. The key lesson is that effective chunking depends heavily on the interplay between the corpus and the types of questions posed. Future work will involve testing embedding models with longer context windows to explore these effects further.

12.5 Conclusions and Future Directions

The research confirms that Retrieval-Augmented Generation systems offer distinct advantages for scholarly work. They successfully integrate verbatim corpora and specialised knowledge, which dramatically reduces model hallucinations. Crucially, their ability to cite relevant documents makes the RAG framework exceptionally well-suited to assisting with scientific tasks. However, their effective deployment comes with two important cautions derived from the authors’ experience.

First, RAG systems are not ‘plug-and-play’; they require considerable adjustment. The optimal settings for retrieval and generation depend intimately on the specific corpus and the types of questions posed. Second, rigorous evaluation is crucial. This demands a representative set of questions and expected answers, a process where the involvement of domain experts, such as philosophers, is indispensable.

Several open challenges remain. When the system fails to find relevant documents, answer quality degrades, highlighting the need for prompts that can gracefully handle this scenario. Furthermore, RAG systems often perform poorly on broad overview questions. The likely reason is that the retrieved local information, which is injected into the prompt, distracts the model from the high-level synthesis required for a global perspective. To address these limitations, the future lies in developing more flexible, ‘agentic RAG systems’ that can discern between different question types and adapt their strategy accordingly.
