Overview

This report details an ERC-funded research project, ActDisease (Acting out Disease), which investigates the historical contributions of patient organisations to modern medicine in 20th-century Europe. Researchers specifically examine how these organisations shaped disease concepts, illness experiences, and medical practices. The project focuses on ten European patient organisations from Sweden, Germany, France, and Great Britain, spanning approximately 1890 to 1990. The primary source material comprises a private, recently digitised collection of patient organisation periodicals, predominantly magazines, totalling 96,186 pages.

A key challenge in processing this diverse material involves the accurate classification of text genres within these periodicals. Despite successful Optical Character Recognition (OCR) using ABBYY FineReader Server 14, complex layouts, slanted text, rare fonts, and varying scan quality persist as issues. These factors lead to OCR errors, particularly in German and French texts, and disrupted reading order. Traditional topic models and term counts prove insufficient for fine-grained analysis due to the co-occurrence of multiple text types on single pages.

Consequently, the project employs genre classification to enable a more nuanced exploration of the material. This approach facilitates historical arguments and comparisons of communicative strategies across countries, diseases, and publications over time. Furthermore, it permits fine-grained analysis of term distributions and topic models within specific genres.

Given the scarcity of annotated data, the project explores both zero-shot and few-shot learning paradigms. For zero-shot classification, researchers investigate the efficacy of mapping genre labels from publicly available modern datasets—including the Corpus of Online Registers of English (CORE), Functional Text Dimensions (FTD) dataset, and UD-MULTIGENRE (UDM)—to custom genre labels defined by the project’s historians. Multilingual encoders such as XLM-Roberta, mBERT, and historical mBERT serve as the primary models, with fine-tuning conducted across various language and balancing configurations.

Few-shot learning experiments assess performance changes with different training set sizes and the impact of prior Masked Language Model (MLM) fine-tuning on the full ActDisease dataset. Additionally, the project explores few-shot prompting of Large Language Models (LLMs), specifically Llama-3.1 8b Instruct, to evaluate its performance with limited examples.

Initial findings indicate that fine-tuning on the FTD dataset, with the project’s specific genre mapping, yields superior zero-shot performance. Certain models demonstrate particular strengths for specific genres; for instance, XLM-Roberta performs well for QA, and historical mBERT excels with administrative texts. In few-shot learning, prior MLM fine-tuning significantly enhances performance, with historical mBERT-MLM outperforming other models, especially in distinguishing fiction from non-fiction. However, challenges remain in differentiating closely related genres like fiction and non-fictional prose, which often share themes in patient experience narratives.

Ultimately, genre classification emerges as a vital tool for making rich, multi-genre historical sources accessible for text mining, enabling deeper comparative historical analysis. The research demonstrates the viability of leveraging modern datasets for general-purpose categories and the potential of open generative models. It also highlights the superior performance of few-shot learning with multilingual encoders, particularly historical mBERT, when combined with prior MLM fine-tuning. Future work includes developing a new, more fine-grained annotation scheme, generating synthetic data, and implementing active learning strategies to further refine classification quality.

5.1 Project Outline and Objectives

This procedural report details experiments in genre classification for historical medical periodicals, undertaken as part of the ActDisease project. The presentation systematically addresses three core areas. Firstly, it introduces the ActDisease project, including its dataset and associated digitisation challenges. Secondly, it provides a comprehensive overview of the genre classification experiments, encompassing their motivation, zero-shot and few-shot learning approaches, and the application of Llama-3.1 prompting. Finally, it summarises the conclusions drawn from the research.

5.2 The ActDisease Project: Scope and Source Material

The ActDisease project, an ERC-funded research initiative, meticulously examines the historical trajectories of patient organisations across 20th-century Europe. Its central objective involves scrutinising how these organisations profoundly influenced the conceptualisation of diseases, the lived experience of illness, and the evolution of medical practices. The research specifically concentrates on ten prominent European patient organisations, originating from Sweden, Germany, France, and Great Britain, with a historical scope spanning approximately 1890 to 1990. Crucially, the project relies upon patient organisation periodicals, primarily magazines, as its principal source material. An illustrative historical image, depicting Heligoland, Germany, around 1890-1900, marks the foundational site of the Hay Fever Association of Heligoland in 1897, underscoring the project’s historical depth.

5.3 The ActDisease Dataset: Digitised Periodicals

The ActDisease project leverages a substantial, recently digitised collection of patient organisation magazines as its core dataset. This private archive encompasses 96,186 pages of material, drawn from various European countries, including Germany, Sweden, France, and the United Kingdom. The collection spans a range of diseases, specifically covering Allergy/Asthma, Diabetes, and Multiple Sclerosis in Germany; Allergy/Asthma, Diabetes, and Lung Diseases in Sweden; Diabetes and Rheumatism/Paralysis in France; and Diabetes and Rheumatism in the UK. Visual examples, such as the covers of “BRA Review” and “Allergia”, illustrate the diverse nature of these historical periodicals.

5.4 Digitisation Process and Persistent Challenges

The digitisation of the ActDisease dataset employed ABBYY FineReader Server 14 for Optical Character Recognition (OCR), which generally performed well on standard layouts and common fonts. Nevertheless, significant challenges persist, particularly with complex page designs, text presented at an angle, and the presence of unusual or rare fonts. Furthermore, variations in the quality of original scans and photographs introduce additional hurdles. These factors collectively contribute to persistent issues, notably OCR errors—which are particularly prevalent in German and French texts—and disrupted reading order within the digitised documents.

Researchers have actively addressed some of these challenges, conducting specific experiments on post-OCR correction for German texts, utilising instruction-tuned generative models. A notable observation reveals that OCR errors frequently occur in creative text segments, such as advertisements, humour pages, and poetry.

5.5 Motivation for Genre Classification in Historical Periodicals

Initial examinations of the ActDisease materials revealed a profound diversity in text types, a characteristic consistently observed across all magazines. Crucially, distinct text types frequently co-exist on a single page; for instance, an administrative report might appear alongside an advertisement or a humour section. This inherent textual heterogeneity poses a significant challenge for conventional analytical methods. Traditional yearly and decade-based topic models, alongside simple term counts, fail to account for this co-occurrence, leading to a probable bias towards the most prevalent text types.

Consequently, genre emerged as an exceptionally useful conceptual framework for distinguishing between these varied texts. Within Language Technology, genre is precisely defined as a class of documents united by a shared communicative purpose, a definition that aligns perfectly with the project’s needs. The ability to classify genre directly supports the project’s core objective: to explore the historical data from multiple perspectives, thereby facilitating robust historical arguments. Specifically, genre classification enables the systematic study of evolving communicative strategies over time, allowing for comparative analysis across different countries, diseases, and publications. Moreover, it permits a more granular analysis of term distributions and topic models, revealing nuanced patterns within specific genre groups.

5.6 Illustrative Genre Examples from ActDisease Data

To illustrate the textual diversity within the ActDisease dataset, several distinct genre examples emerge. The collection includes instances of poetry, demonstrating the creative content present in these historical periodicals. Academic reports, such as those detailing studies on the pancreas, also feature prominently, conveying scientific and medical information. Formal legal documents, exemplified by a deed of covenant, highlight the administrative and regulatory aspects of patient organisations. Commercial content appears in the form of advertisements, for instance, promoting chocolate for diabetics.

Furthermore, the magazines contain instructive messages and guidance, ranging from recipes to dietary advice. Reports from patient organisations themselves, detailing their meetings and activities, provide insight into their operational aspects. Finally, the periodicals frequently feature narratives recounting patient experiences, offering personal perspectives on illness and daily life.

5.7 Genre Classification Experiment Design

Confronted with a scarcity of annotated data, researchers strategically explored both zero-shot and few-shot learning paradigms for genre classification. The zero-shot learning investigation sought to determine whether an efficient mapping of genre labels from existing public datasets to the project’s custom labels was feasible, whilst also assessing performance variations across different datasets and models. Conversely, the few-shot learning inquiry focused on understanding how classification performance changes with varying training data sizes across models. A further crucial question addressed whether prior fine-tuning on the entire dataset could substantially improve performance. This experimental design underpins the findings presented in the referenced work by Danilova and Söderfeldt [Danilova & Söderfeldt, Year].

5.8 Genre Label Definition and Categories

The genre labels employed in this classification task were meticulously defined under the direct supervision of the project’s lead historian, an expert in patient organisation studies. This collaborative approach ensured the labels’ utility for segmenting content, thereby facilitating deeper historical analysis of the source materials. Furthermore, the labels were deliberately crafted to be as general-purpose as possible, enhancing the reusability of the classifier on similar datasets.

Nine distinct genres were established, each with a precise definition: * ‘Academic’ aims to convey scientific and medical information from the community to the magazine’s readership. * ‘Administrative’ focuses on reporting and informing about patient organisation events and activities. * ‘Advertisement’ encompasses commercial promotions. * ‘Guide’ provides instructive messages or advice. * ‘Fiction’ denotes creative narratives. * ‘Legal’ refers to formal legal documents. * ‘News’ covers reports on current events. * ‘Nonfiction Prose’ includes general prose, often biographical or descriptive. * Finally, ‘QA’ specifically designates question-answering sections found within the periodicals.

5.9 Annotation Methodology and Inter-Annotator Agreement

The annotation process adopted paragraphs as its fundamental unit, derived from ABBYY OCR output and subsequently merged based on consistent font patterns—including type, size, bolding, and italicisation—at the page level. Researchers sampled content from two key periodicals: the Swedish “Diabetes” and the German “Diabetiker Journal”, specifically selecting first and mid-year issues from each publication year. A team of six project members, comprising four historians and two computational linguists, undertook the annotation, ensuring proficiency in both Swedish and German. Each paragraph received two independent annotations, yielding an impressive average inter-annotator agreement of 0.95, as measured by Krippendorff’s alpha. An illustrative example of the annotation interface, a .numbers file, demonstrates the structured input, featuring columns for metadata such as year and title, alongside binary assignments for each genre category.

5.10 Dataset Splitting for Experiments

For the experimental phase, the annotated data underwent a primary split into training and held-out sets. The training set comprised 1182 paragraphs, whilst the held-out set contained 552 paragraphs, representing approximately 30% of the total annotated data. Both sets were meticulously stratified by genre label to ensure representative distributions.

Within the few-shot experiments, researchers prepared six distinct training set sizes: 100, 200, 300, 400, 500, and the full 1182 paragraphs. These subsets were randomly sampled from the main training set, maintaining a balanced distribution across labels. The held-out set was further subdivided into equally sized validation and test sets, also balanced by label. Notably, the ‘Legal’ and ‘News’ genres were excluded from these experiments due to insufficient training data. Conversely, the zero-shot experiments leveraged the entire test set for evaluation.

5.11 Genre Distribution Across Datasets

Visualisations of the genre distribution across both the training and held-out samples of the ActDisease dataset reveal specific patterns. Bar charts meticulously plot the counts for various genres—including QA, academic, administrative, advertisement, fiction, guide, legal, news, and non-fiction—segmented by German and Swedish languages. A notable observation from these plots highlights a significant imbalance in the distribution of ‘Advertisement’ and ‘Non-fictional Prose’ categories when compared across the two languages.

5.12 External Datasets for Zero-Shot Experiments

For the zero-shot experiments, researchers incorporated several external datasets, drawing from prior work in automatic web genre classification. The Corpus of Online Registers of English (CORE), developed by Egbert et al. [Egbert et al., 2015], provided English data annotated at the document level, with main categories also available in Swedish, Finnish, and French. The Functional Text Dimensions (FTD) dataset, introduced by Sharoff [Sharoff, 2018], offered a balanced collection of web genres in both English and Russian, having been widely utilised in previous automatic web genre classification studies, including by Kuzman et al. [Kuzman et al., 2023]. Additionally, a sample from Universal Dependencies, known as UD-MULTIGENRE (UDM) by de Marneffe et al. [de Marneffe et al., 2021], proved valuable. This dataset encompasses genre annotations at the sentence level across 38 languages, with genre information specifically recovered by Danilova and Stymne [Danilova & Stymne, 2023].

5.13 Cross-Dataset Genre Mapping

The crucial process of genre mapping across the various datasets involved independent assignments by two distinct annotators. To ensure the highest level of consistency and reliability, only those assignments demonstrating full agreement between the annotators were selected for the final mapping. A significant observation from this process revealed that for certain specific genres within the ActDisease dataset, no directly suitable or equivalent labels existed within the publicly available external datasets.

5.14 Training Data Creation Pipeline

The training data creation pipeline initiates with a genre map, which guides the mapping process across the CORE, FTD, and UDM datasets. This step generates a DataFrame incorporating the newly mapped genres. Subsequently, a rigorous pre-processing phase removes extraneous elements such as web addresses, email addresses, XML-tags, and emoji, ensuring data cleanliness. The cleaned data then undergoes a sampling procedure, configured according to specific options. These configurations include ‘G+’ for Germanic languages only, ‘G-’ for all language families, ‘B1’ for balancing by the ActDisease project’s custom labels, and ‘B2’ for balancing by both the ActDisease and original dataset labels. This systematic approach ultimately yields four distinct training samples for each of the FTD, CORE, and UDM datasets.

5.15 Multilingual Encoder Models for Classification

For the classification experiments, researchers employed a suite of multilingual encoder models, each demonstrating proven efficacy in previous genre classification tasks. The selection included XLM-Roberta, introduced by Conneau et al. [Conneau et al., 2020], and mBERT, developed by Devlin et al. [Devlin et al., 2019]. Crucially, the historical mBERT model, published by Schweter et al. [Schweter et al., 2022], offered particular relevance, having been pre-trained on an extensive corpus of multilingual historical newspapers that encompasses the project’s target languages. mBERT served as a comparative baseline for hmBERT, given the challenges in directly comparing its performance with XLM-Roberta.

These BERT-like models have been widely adopted in prior research for web register and genre classification, notably by Lepekhin and Sharoff [Lepekhin & Sharoff, 2022], Kuzman and Ljubešić [Kuzman & Ljubešić, 2023], and Laippala et al. [Laippala et al., 2023]. XLM-RoBERTa, in particular, stands out as a state-of-the-art web genre classifier, as highlighted by Kuzman et al. [Kuzman et al., 2023]. The fine-tuning process involved applying the FTD, CORE, UDM, and merged datasets to XLM-Roberta, mBERT, and hmBERT. Each training set was processed across four distinct configurations, ultimately yielding a total of 48 fine-tuned models. All reported metrics represent averages across these configurations, ensuring a robust evaluation.

5.16 Zero-Shot Learning Evaluation Methodology

Evaluating zero-shot predictions presented a methodological challenge due to the imperfect overlap of genre label sets across the various datasets. Consequently, direct comparisons of overall performance metrics proved unfeasible. Instead, researchers meticulously assessed the performance of each genre independently, complementing this analysis with detailed examinations of confusion matrices to mitigate potential biases.

The X-GENRE web genre classifier, a state-of-the-art tool by Kuzman et al. [Kuzman et al., 2023], served as a crucial baseline. Predictions from this baseline were specifically considered only for the most similar labels that could be directly mapped to the ActDisease project’s custom genres. The evaluation also accounted for cross-lingual scenarios: the FTD and X-GENRE models operated in a fully cross-lingual context, lacking German or Swedish training data, whilst the UDM and CORE datasets exhibited partially cross-lingual characteristics.

5.17 Overview of Zero-Shot Classification Results

An overarching analysis of the zero-shot classification results reveals distinct performance patterns across datasets. Models fine-tuned on the Functional Text Dimensions (FTD) dataset, when combined with the ActDisease project’s specific genre mapping, consistently demonstrate superior performance. In most configurations, these models exhibit no discernible class-specific bias, and their metrics per genre are robust.

Conversely, other datasets introduce class-specific biases. The UD-MULTIGENRE (UDM) dataset, for instance, shows a bias towards the ‘news’ genre, attributable to the highest number of Germanic instances—predominantly German—within its news training data. Similarly, the Corpus of Online Registers of English (CORE) exhibits a bias towards the ‘guide’ genre, as its ‘guide’ training data uniquely features multilingual content.

Intriguingly, certain models demonstrate particular strengths on specific datasets. XLM-Roberta, when fine-tuned on UDM, achieves an average of 32% more correct predictions in the ‘QA’ (Question-Answering) genre compared to mBERT and hmBERT. Conversely, hmBERT, also fine-tuned on UDM, yields an average of 16% more correct predictions in the ‘Administrative’ genre than XLM-Roberta and mBERT. Furthermore, models trained on the CORE dataset consistently perform well in predicting the ‘legal’ genre.

5.20 Few-Shot Learning Performance Analysis

An analysis of few-shot learning performance, visualised through a line graph, illustrates the F1 scores achieved by various models—hmBERT, hmBERT-MLM, mBERT, mBERT-MLM, XLMR, and XLMR-MLM—as a function of dataset size. A clear advantage emerges for models that undergo further training on the ActDisease dataset, denoted by the ‘+MLM’ suffix, indicating prior Masked Language Model (MLM) fine-tuning. The F1 score consistently improves with an increasing number of training instances, demonstrating a positive correlation between data volume and performance. Nevertheless, even with the largest training size of 1182 instances, the F1 score remains below 0.8. Notably, hmBERT-MLM consistently outperforms all other models, albeit by a small margin, showcasing the benefits of its historical pre-training combined with additional fine-tuning.

5.21 Few-Shot Learning: Per-Category F1 and Overall Metrics

Detailed examination of the few-shot learning results reveals per-category F1 scores and overall metrics for XLMR, XLMR-MLM, hmBERT, hmBERT-MLM, mBERT, and mBERT-MLM at training sizes of 500 and 1182 instances. These metrics span various genres, including QA, academic, administrative, advertisement, fiction, guide, and non-fiction prose, alongside overall accuracy, macro F1, and weighted average scores. A critical insight emerges: hmBERT-MLM’s superior performance largely stems from its sustained ability to differentiate between fiction and non-fiction genres, even when trained on the full dataset. In stark contrast, other models, particularly XLM-Roberta, exhibit a significant decline in performance for these specific categories, underscoring hmBERT-MLM’s unique strength in this regard.

5.22 Few-Shot Performance Analysis: Genre Similarity

A detailed analysis of the few-shot performance, particularly examining the confusion matrix for XLM-Roberta-MLM with the full-sized training dataset, reveals a notable pattern: non-fictional prose is frequently misclassified as fiction. This misprediction likely arises because both genres within the ActDisease dataset commonly feature narratives centred on patient experiences. Researchers hypothesise that as the training data size increases, these two genres become more semantically similar to the model, necessitating additional data to enhance differentiation.

Furthermore, the inherent domain specificity of the source material—patient organisation magazines exclusively focused on diabetes—means that all genres are confined to this particular context. Consequently, both fictional and autobiographical texts frequently revolve around the experiences of diabetes patients, leading to a probable overlap in themes and narrative structures, which further complicates precise genre distinction.

5.23 Few-Shot Prompting with Llama-3.1 8b Instruct

Given the current insufficiency of data for comprehensive instruction tuning, researchers opted for a few-shot prompting approach utilising Llama-3.1 8b Instruct, a widely recognised multilingual generative model with open weights. The prompt structure was meticulously designed to guide the model effectively. It commenced with a clear instruction: “Label the text with one of the following genres based on their purpose and content:”, followed by a comprehensive list of the defined genres, each accompanied by two or three carefully selected examples. The input text for classification was then presented within a specific format: [INST] Input: test[i]["text"] [/INST] Genre:, ensuring consistent interaction with the model.

5.24 Llama-3.1 8b Instruct Results

The evaluation of Llama-3.1 8b Instruct’s few-shot prediction performance, conducted on the entire held-out set designated as the zero-shot test set, yielded mixed results. A table presents F1-scores for various genres, alongside overall metrics including accuracy, macro average, and weighted average. A corresponding confusion matrix visually depicts the true versus predicted genre classifications. Whilst the model demonstrated a reasonable capability in handling certain genre labels, the limited provision of two or three examples per genre proved insufficient for robust representation of categories such as non-fictional prose, advertisement, and administrative texts. This limitation suggests that more comprehensive exemplars are required for these complex genres.

5.25 Key Conclusions from Genre Classification

Popular magazines emerge as a highly promising source for research within the history of science, yet their inherent richness in diverse genres presents a significant challenge for text mining, often surpassing the complexity encountered with scientific journals and books. These varied genres crucially reflect the deliberate choices of communicative strategies employed by authors and organisations. Consequently, acknowledging and accounting for this genre diversity proves essential for accurate and detailed interpretation of text mining results. Genre classification, therefore, serves as a pivotal tool, rendering these complex historical sources accessible for advanced text mining and enabling comparative analyses from novel perspectives.

When confronted with a lack of dedicated training data, two viable options present themselves. Firstly, leveraging existing modern datasets can prove successful, particularly if the target categories or labels are general-purpose. Secondly, few-shot instruction of a robust generative model offers a pathway to achieve decent classification quality. However, for the ActDisease project, the most effective approach involved few-shot learning with multilingual encoders, augmented by prior Masked Language Model (MLM) fine-tuning. This method yielded the most substantial gains, particularly for the historical multilingual BERT model, which demonstrated a remarkable 24% improvement, significantly outperforming mBERT-MLM’s 14.5% gain and XLM-RoBERTa’s 16.9% gain.

5.26 Ongoing and Future Work

The project’s ongoing and future work focuses on several key areas designed to enhance both the quality and utility of the research for the academic community. Researchers are currently engaged in applying the developed methodologies to specific historical hypotheses, aiming to derive concrete insights from the classified data. A significant undertaking involves the development of a new annotation scheme, which will introduce more fine-grained genre distinctions. This annotation project has secured funding from Swe-CLARIN, underscoring its importance. Furthermore, methodological advancements are being pursued through the exploration of synthetic data generation and the implementation of active learning strategies, both poised to refine and improve the classification process.
