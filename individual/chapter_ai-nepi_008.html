Overview

This chapter meticulously details a novel approach to historical and scientific inquiry. The authors leverage advanced AI models whilst addressing the inherent limitations of current Large Language Models (LLMs). Their core objective involves establishing a robust framework for “Computational Epistemology,” a new discipline focused on validating propositions, arguments, and evidence within scholarly contexts. The authors contend that contemporary LLMs, despite their rapid evolution—from “Attention is all you need” to “Thinking is all you need”—lack crucial capabilities. These include countering hallucinations, discerning true meaning from embedding vectors, prioritising justified knowledge over popular falsehoods, and formulating effective inquiry plans.

To bridge these critical gaps, the proposed system champions “validation” as the paramount requirement. It aims to provide reasons, arguments, and evidence for propositions, alongside justifications for actions, thereby fostering genuine “epistemic agency.” This necessitates the identification of propositions beyond mere sentences, the extraction of arguments from texts, and the reconstruction of intentions, plans, and actions of historical figures from documented traces.

The practical implementation centres on a sophisticated working environment, exemplified by the Cursor platform, which integrates multimodal LLMs such as Gemini 2.5, Claude, and Llama. An AI agent, named Bernoulli by the research team, operates within this environment, enabling complex historical inquiries, such as reconstructing the involvement of Leonhard Euler in the construction failures at Sanssouci castle.

The infrastructure comprises five essential components:

  • Firstly, a scholarly curated editorial board oversees the validation of primary sources, including monumental works like the Opera Omnia Euler.

  • Secondly, a “Scholarium” registry serves as a structured database of content items—encompassing personal chronologies, communication acts, and the historical use of terminology, tools, and data—critically serving as a validated substitute for embedding-based approaches.

  • Thirdly, an AI Cockpit user interface facilitates interaction with the integrated LLMs.

  • Fourthly, a FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) data infrastructure, specifically Zenodo hosted by CERN, ensures the long-term preservation and publication of research data.

  • Finally, Open Science Technology, a dedicated startup, provides technical support, including an MCP API server, promoting open source, open access, open data, and fostering global open collaboration for standardised AI access to knowledge.

This comprehensive framework directly addresses the challenges of maintaining scholarly expertise in the digital age, ensuring data longevity, and overcoming the limitations of current AI in delivering complete and historically validated answers.

8.1 Evolution of Large Language Models and Their Limitations

Large Language Models have undergone a remarkably swift evolution. They progressed from the foundational principle of “Attention is all you need” to the widespread adoption of “Chat with GPT.” Subsequently, the emphasis shifted towards “Context is all you need,” often augmented by Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) techniques. The latest models now propose that “Thinking is all you need,” signifying the current frontier of LLM development.

Nevertheless, the authors observe that significant capabilities remain absent from these advanced models. Crucially, they lack an inherent “opponent” mechanism to counter hallucinations, a pervasive issue in AI-generated content. Furthermore, embedding vectors, whilst useful for semantic similarity, do not genuinely represent the meanings of expressions. Consequently, LLMs frequently formulate statements that, whilst superficially appealing, prove factually incorrect. They also tend to replicate information disseminated via internet media, rather than discerning and prioritising genuinely justified knowledge. Beyond these shortcomings, current models cannot effectively seek what is best justified nor can they formulate optimal plans for scientific inquiry.

Present technological limitations mean no existing model can achieve these critical objectives, even to a minimal degree. Indeed, current technologies offer no discernible path towards realising these missing goals.

8.2 The Imperative of Validation: Introducing Computational Epistemology

A fundamental requirement for advancing AI capabilities in scholarly contexts centres on validation. Indeed, validation proves indispensable. This process furnishes reasons, arguments, and evidence both for and against the truth of a given proposition. Moreover, it provides justifications for or against the pursuit of specific actions.

To address this critical need, the authors propose a new discipline: computational epistemology. This field specifically concerns itself with the methods and methodologies required to bridge the existing validation gap. It aims to cultivate “epistemic agency” within AI systems. Achieving this agency necessitates the ability to identify propositions that extend beyond mere sentences, to discern and extract arguments embedded within texts, sources, and historical inquiries, and to accurately identify the intentions, plans, and actions of individuals as documented and traced through historical records.

8.3 Practical Application: Historical Inquiry Workflow

The research team has developed a functional working environment to facilitate historical inquiry. A screenshot of this interface reveals an open historical source on the left-hand side—specifically, a book titled “Dem Allerdurchlauchtigsten, Großmächtigsten König und Herrn Friedrich Wilhelm dem Zweiten König von Preußen.” The right-hand panel, labelled “Personen und Aufgaben in der Bibliothek” (Persons and tasks in the library), presents the inquiry interface.

Within this environment, users can formulate precise questions, such as “Rekonstruiere welche Personen an der Wasserfontaine welche Arbeiten ausführten” (Reconstruct which persons performed which work on the water fountain). This particular query relates to the historical context of Sanssouci castle’s construction under Frederick the Great, a project notable for one of the 18th century’s most significant construction failures. Historians of science have long debated the extent of Leonhard Euler’s involvement and culpability in this failure, an open question that this AI-assisted inquiry seeks to address. The system aims to provide a validated, qualified, and reliable answer, grounded in proven evidence rather than mere hearsay. The output typically comprises a detailed list of individuals, their contributions, work dates, payments received, and their successes or failures.

The technical infrastructure underpinning this system includes a dedicated inquiry window within the Cursor environment, a versatile platform designed for AI agents. An AI agent, presumptuously named Bernoulli by the authors, conducts these complex historical investigations. A significant challenge, however, extends beyond merely reading individual PDF sources; the system must search all available sources, as simple indexing or token concentration proves insufficient for comprehensive historical analysis.

8.4 Scholarly Curation of Evidence

The research infrastructure incorporates a crucial first component: a scholarly curated editorial board responsible for validating and preparing primary sources. This rigorous oversight ensures the integrity and reliability of the foundational data. Exemplifying this commitment to scholarly excellence, the Opera Omnia of Euler stands as a monumental achievement. This collection, comprising 86 volumes, represents 120 years of dedicated scholarly work by numerous experts. Its comprehensive editing, completed just two years prior, encompasses all of Euler’s letters and his 866 published works.

Beyond Euler’s oeuvre, the authors integrate other foundational scholarly collections, including the Kepler Gesammelte Werke and the Brahe Opera Omnia, further enriching their evidential base. A visual representation of this meticulous curation appears in a screenshot of the “LEONHARD EULER BRIEFWECHSEL OPERA OMNIA SERIES QUARTA A: COMMERCIUM EPISTOLICUM VOL. I HERAUSGEBER A. JUSKEVIČ” webpage, highlighting the detailed organisation of these invaluable historical documents.

8.5 The Scholarium Registry: A Substitute for Embeddings

A pivotal and innovative component of the system is the “Scholarium” registry, which serves as a direct substitute for conventional embedding-based approaches. This Scholarium functions as a meticulously curated database of content items, designed by the authors to provide a highly detailed inventory of historically proven activities.

The registry organises information into various chronologies, capturing a comprehensive range of historical data. These include chronologies of personal actions, statements, and the implications, arguments, and inquiries made by individuals. Furthermore, it meticulously documents the historical use of language, terminology, and concepts, alongside the application of specific concepts, relations, models, and methods. The registry also tracks the utilisation of tools, devices, data, information, evidence, and sources. Each entry undergoes rigorous validation against original historical sources, ensuring the integrity and accuracy of the documented activities. The system also integrates with the MCP API, as indicated by a screenshot of “ANTHROPIC Introducing the Model Context Protocol,” signifying its adherence to modern API standards for AI interaction.

8.6 User Interface and Multimodal Model Integration

The system’s third key component is its user interface, termed the “AI Cockpit.” This interface allows researchers to interact with the meticulously curated historical records using readily accessible multimodal models. The developers have found that employing multimodal models optimally addresses the complex requirements of historical inquiry tasks.

Specifically, the system integrates leading models such as Claude, Gemini (including the latest Gemini 2.5 iteration), and Llama. The distinct advantage of these multimodal models lies in their capacity to seamlessly combine information derived from both text and images, a crucial feature for comprehensive historical analysis. The entire platform operates within the LettreAI framework on Cursor, providing a consistent and powerful working environment, visually similar to the inquiry panel previously shown in the presentation.

8.7 FAIR Data Infrastructure

A critical fourth component establishes a robust FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) repository for the long-term storage and publication of research data. The authors have selected Zenodo for this purpose, a platform hosted by CERN in Geneva. This choice ensures the enduring availability of the data for many years, supporting the principles of open science. A screenshot of the Zenodo website illustrates its functionality, displaying “Featured communities” and recent uploads, including a dataset for a journal article on “Wide spectral range optical characterization of terbium gallium garnet (TGG) single crystal by universal dispersion mode.”

8.8 Technical Support and Open Collaboration

The final essential component involves dedicated technical support, provided by Open Science Technology, a startup committed to the principles of Open Source, Open Access, and Open Data. This organisation manages the operational infrastructure, including an MCP API Server.

The MCP API Server plays a crucial role in standardising AI access APIs to knowledge globally, thereby enabling worldwide inquiries by various artificial intelligence models. This initiative represents a significant endeavour to establish a universal standard for AI interaction with scholarly knowledge, fostering an environment of open collaboration across the international research community.
