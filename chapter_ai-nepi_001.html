Overview

This workshop convenes experts to explore the application of large language models (LLMs) within the history, philosophy, and sociology of science. The event, organised by Adrian Wüthrich, Arno Simons, Michael Zich, and Gerd Grasshoff, has attracted considerable attention, with over 50 paper submissions and more than 220 registered participants attending both in person and online.

The workshop’s conception arises from two principal sources: the Network Epistemology in Practice (NEPI) project and Gerd Grasshoff’s sustained advocacy for artificial intelligence in the history and philosophy of science. Within the NEPI project, Arno Simons has pioneered the training of LLMs on physics texts, whilst Michael Zich has applied these models to analyse conceptual challenges in physics. Concurrently, Gerd Grasshoff has long championed the use of AI to examine the processes of scientific discovery.

Funded by the ERC Grant Network Epistemology in Practice, the workshop directly advances the NEPI project’s central objective: to meticulously study the internal communications of the ATLAS collaboration at CERN. This research seeks to illuminate how one of the world’s largest particle physics collaborations collectively generates new knowledge. To this end, the project integrates network analysis for mapping communication structures with semantic tools, including LLMs, to trace the flow of ideas.

The organisers extend their sincere gratitude to Svenja Götz, Lea Stengel, and Julia Kim for their invaluable administrative and organisational support. Thanks are also due to Oliver Ziegler and the Unicam team for their technical expertise in recording the sessions and managing the Zoom meeting.

2.1 Logistics and Recording

All workshop sessions are being recorded, a condition to which participants consented during registration. The recording setup consists of a single camera focused on the presenter and four microphones, with an iPhone serving as an audio backup.

With the explicit consent of each speaker, videos of the talks—including both the presentation and subsequent discussion—will be made available on the NEPI YouTube Channel. During discussion periods, the recording will capture only the presenter’s audio and video. Participants may approach the organisers for further information or to withdraw their consent.

To manage time effectively with a large audience, attendees are kindly asked to keep questions and comments concise. The organisers will collate approximately four questions after each talk for the presenter to address as a group.

For ongoing dialogue, participants may use the provided Etherpad (Cryptpad) to post comments and questions. Additionally, the Zoom chat facilitates real-time interaction for all attendees. The programme also includes scheduled lunch and coffee breaks, a reception, and a workshop dinner to encourage informal networking. Please note that seating for the dinner is limited.

2.3 Keynote 1: Cultural and Societal Change

The first keynote, titled Large-scale text analysis for the study of cultural and societal change, will be presented by Pierluigi Cassotti and Nina Tahmasebi of the University of Gothenburg.

Within the ‘Change is Key’ research programme, Nina Tahmasebi serves as Principal Investigator and Pierluigi Cassotti contributes as a researcher. Both are known for their influential work in semantic change detection.

Their research agenda is notably comprehensive, addressing not only the technical development of benchmarks for evaluating analytical methods but also the broader methodological challenges of applying data science to complex questions in the humanities. This interdisciplinary focus makes their work particularly relevant to the themes of this workshop.

2.4 Keynote 2: Cross-Document Analysis

The second keynote, scheduled for tomorrow afternoon, will be delivered by Iryna Gurevych. Her presentation is titled How to InterText? Elevating NLP to the cross-document level.

Professor Gurevych is the director of the Ubiquitous Knowledge Processing (UKP) Lab at the Technical University Darmstadt. Her research portfolio is extensive, covering information extraction, advanced semantic text processing, and machine learning methods.

Of particular importance to this workshop, her work also involves the direct application of Natural Language Processing techniques to challenges within the social sciences and humanities, reinforcing the event’s interdisciplinary spirit.
